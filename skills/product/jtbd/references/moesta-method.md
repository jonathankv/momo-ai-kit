# Bob Moesta JTBD Method — Deep Reference

> Source: Bob Moesta episodes on Lenny's Podcast (ep.1 + ep.2), verified against transcripts

## Core Principle
"People hire products to make progress in their life." Products don't have jobs — people have jobs. Study the struggling moment, not the product.

## Four Forces of Progress

### F1: Push of the Situation
The context pushing someone away from their current state. Has NOTHING to do with the new product.
- Examples: "I didn't eat lunch", "the old car had 280,000 miles", "3 large bills in 4 months"
- Without push, they can't even SEE your product (creatures of habit)

### F2: Pull of the New Solution
Attraction to a new outcome/state. The idea of what's possible.
- "I want to get back to work as fast as possible" (Snickers)
- "I want to regroup after an emotional experience" (Milky Way)

### F3: Anxiety of the New
Every time you show something new, it creates anxiety. More features = more anxiety.
- "Can it really do all those things?"
- Learning curve, switching cost, risk of failure

### F4: Habit of the Present
Comfort, familiarity, sunk cost of current solution.
- Condo example: people cancelled 6 weeks later because "didn't know how to get rid of stuff"
- Fix: raised price, included moving + 2yr storage → +30% sales (reduced F4)

### The Inequality
`(F1+F2) > (F3+F4)` → change happens
- Most companies try to increase F2 (more features, more pull)
- Often better: decrease F3/F4 (reduce friction, reduce anxiety)

## Three Energy Dimensions
Every force operates across 3 dimensions:
1. **Functional:** time, space, effort, knowledge, workflow
2. **Emotional:** how I feel — frustrated, relieved, confident, overwhelmed
3. **Social:** how others perceive me — boss, peers, family

A job pathway should show signals in at least 2/3 dimensions.

## Interview Method

### Who to Interview
- People who ALREADY bought/switched/churned
- NOT people who "want to" or "plan to"
- "Bitchin' ain't switchin'" — complaining ≠ action
- For zero-to-one: interview people using competitors/adjacent solutions ("What will people STOP using when your product comes out?")

### Sample Size
- 7-8 interviews: patterns start repeating
- 10 interviews: usually sufficient
- Max 12 per round
- 2 rounds of 12 > 1 round of 24

### Technique (not a discussion guide)
Use F1-F4 as compass, not a script. Follow the story.

**Opening:** "Tell me the story of the day you decided to [buy/switch/leave]..."

**3 Layers of Language:**
1. **Pablum:** "It was good." → Push: "What was good about it?"
2. **Fantasy/Nightmare:** "It was AMAZING!" → Ground: "What specifically happened?"
3. **Actual Story:** "Here's what went down..." → This is truth. Dig here.

**Key Techniques:**
- Play back incorrectly → they say "no" → elaborate
- Push to "edge of language" → bracket: "Was it more X or Y?" (force new vocabulary)
- Never ask "why" directly (defensive) — ask "what happened before that?"
- Reference: "Never Split The Difference" by Chris Voss

### Timeline Reconstruction
Reconstruct the full buying timeline:
1. First thought ("something's not right")
2. Passive looking (problem-aware, googling)
3. Active looking (comparing solutions)
4. Deciding (trade-offs, the actual moment)
5. First use (did it deliver?)
6. Ongoing use / churn

People think they decided in a week. Often it was 18 months. Investigate the full timeline.

## 6 Buying Phases (Detail)

| Phase | Customer State | What They Need | Common Mistake |
|-------|---------------|----------------|----------------|
| 1. First thought | Something's not right | Space to recognize the problem | Ignored — no marketing here |
| 2. Passive looking | Problem-aware, solution-unaware | Education, stories, "people like me" | Treated as "lead" too early |
| 3. Active looking | Problem+solution aware | Comparisons, alternatives, "how others decided" | Pushed to demo too fast |
| 4. Deciding | Making trade-offs | Clear choices, reduce anxiety | Only phase most companies optimize |
| 5. First use | "Did it work?" | Easy onboarding, quick win | Abandoned after sale |
| 6. Ongoing use | Building new habit | Reinforcement, habit formation | No re-engagement |

## Clustering (not Segmenting)
Don't look for themes across all interviews. Find PATHWAYS:
- Sets of pushes that co-occur with specific pulls
- Usually 3-5 distinct pathways per product
- Pathways can conflict: one wants speed, another wants thoroughness
- Intercom: discovered 4 jobs, turned off irrelevant features per pathway → changed pricing to match real competitors per job
