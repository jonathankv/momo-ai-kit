# Logical Fallacies — Detection & Diagnosis Guide

10 fallacies most common in business arguments. Use in Mode B (Reasoning Audit).

---

## Quick Detection Test (4 Questions)

Before deep analysis, run this quick screen:

1. **Is the evidence sufficient?** → catches hasty generalization, cherry picking
2. **Is the logic valid?** → catches false cause, slippery slope, false dichotomy
3. **Is the source relevant?** → catches appeal to authority, bandwagon, appeal to tradition
4. **Is the argument addressing the actual point?** → catches straw man, ad hominem

If any answer is "no" → dig into the specific fallacy below.

---

## Fallacy Catalog

| # | Fallacy | What It Is | Business Example | How to Counter |
|---|---------|-----------|-----------------|----------------|
| 1 | **Ad Hominem** | Attacking person instead of argument | "We can't use her idea — she's new to the team" | Evaluate argument quality independent of who presents it |
| 2 | **False Dichotomy** | Presenting only 2 options when more exist | "We either expand globally or risk stagnation" | "What other options exist beyond these two?" |
| 3 | **Straw Man** | Misrepresenting opponent's position to attack it | Distorting "let's be cautious" into "you want to stop investing" | Steelman: find the STRONGEST version of the opposing view |
| 4 | **Post Hoc (False Cause)** | X happened before Y, so X caused Y | "Sales improved after rebrand, so rebrand caused it" | Identify confounding factors; use controlled experiments |
| 5 | **Appeal to Authority** | True because authority endorses it | "This works because [famous CEO] uses it" | Evaluate evidence and relevance to YOUR situation |
| 6 | **Bandwagon** | True because many agree | "Competitors all adopted AI, so we must too" | Does the trend align with YOUR strategic objectives? |
| 7 | **Hasty Generalization** | Broad conclusions from insufficient evidence | "First 3 customers loved it — it'll be a hit" | Representative sample before generalizing |
| 8 | **Slippery Slope** | One action triggers inevitable catastrophe | "If we allow remote work, productivity will collapse" | Demand evidence for EACH step in the causal chain |
| 9 | **Cherry Picking** | Selecting only supporting data | Highlighting revenue growth while hiding rising costs | Examine ALL relevant metrics; use comprehensive datasets |
| 10 | **Appeal to Tradition** | True because "we've always done it" | "We've always done waterfall; agile is a fad" | Evaluate current relevance. Tradition ≠ optimal. |

## Corpus-Sourced Insight

Shreyas Doshi identifies two subtle fallacies particularly common in product culture:

**Alliteration Bias:** We're disproportionately attracted to ideas with catchy names. "Fail fast" sounds better than "fail quickly" — but the idea is identical. The packaging shouldn't influence your evaluation of the idea.

**Authority Bias in Frameworks:** "Two-way door" and "disagree and commit" are powerful ideas, but teams adopt them because Bezos said them, not because they evaluated whether the idea applies to their context. At PM level, most "two-way doors" are actually one-way doors.

> "We get overly excited about cool metaphors... the only difference is you got attracted to the catchy metaphor, and the other one is the authority bias, because Bezos said it." — Shreyas Doshi

## How to Use in Reasoning Audit (Mode B)

1. Run 4-question quick test first
2. If flags found → identify specific fallacy from catalog
3. For each fallacy detected:
   - Name it specifically
   - Quote the exact problematic reasoning
   - Explain why it's fallacious
   - Suggest the steelmanned version or correct reasoning
4. Rate severity: **Critical** (conclusion depends on it) / **Moderate** (weakens but doesn't invalidate) / **Minor** (stylistic, not structural)
