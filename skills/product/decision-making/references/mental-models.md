# Mental Models — Decision-Making & Experimentation

13 models extracted from Lenny's Podcast corpus. Each grounded in source transcript.

---

## The Decision Quality Stack (Synthesized Framework)

Integrates all 13 models into a 4-layer decision process:

```
LAYER 1: CLASSIFY THE DECISION
├── Truly reversible? (Be honest — most aren't at PM level)
├── How much information do we have? (>70% = decide now)
├── What's the cost of delay vs. cost of being wrong?
└── Who is the decider? (DACI/RAPID roles assigned?)

LAYER 2: GATHER INDEPENDENT INPUT
├── Collect opinions asynchronously (never discover in meetings)
├── Make implicit assumptions explicit (write them down)
├── Seek disconfirming evidence (adversarial collaboration)
└── Check: Am I attracted to the idea or to the packaging?

LAYER 3: DECIDE & PRE-COMMIT
├── Define kill criteria + specific actions before launching
├── Identify leading indicators (no long feedback loops)
├── Set review checkpoints (30/60/90 days)
└── The decider decides — others commit

LAYER 4: LEARN & ITERATE
├── Separate decision quality from outcome quality
├── Document surprising outcomes (wins AND losses)
├── Quarterly surprise review (most interesting experiments)
└── Ask: Would I start this today? (sunk cost check)
```

## MM-01: Resulting (Decision Quality ≠ Outcome Quality)
**Source:** Annie Duke — `episodes/annie-duke/transcript.md`

We judge decisions by outcomes rather than process quality. A good decision can produce a bad outcome. Evaluating decisions by outcomes ("resulting") prevents learning.

> "It's so incredibly necessary in improving decision quality to take what's implicit and make it explicit. It's not that intuition is crap, your intuition is sometimes right. If you don't make it explicit, then you don't get to find out when it's wrong." — Annie Duke

**Apply:** Before any retrospective, separate information available at decision time from eventual outcome.

---

## MM-02: Make the Implicit Explicit
**Source:** Annie Duke — `episodes/annie-duke/transcript.md`

Force independent, asynchronous opinion gathering before group discussion. Eliminates anchoring, cross-influence, and loudest-voice-wins.

> "The best way to get somebody's opinion is independently of other people's opinions, independently asynchronously. So the way that I put is I want people to stop talking to each other so much." — Annie Duke

> "I'm not anti your gut or your intuition. I think it's probably sometimes pretty good. I just want you to make it explicit, that's all." — Annie Duke

---

## MM-03: Pre-Mortem + Kill Criteria
**Source:** Annie Duke — `episodes/annie-duke/transcript.md`

A pre-mortem alone doesn't change behavior. It becomes powerful only when paired with pre-committed kill criteria — specific signals that trigger specific actions.

> "So a pre-mortem, it's great only if you set up kill criteria. Commit to actions that you're going to take if you see those signals." — Annie Duke

**Case Study:** Annie Duke + sales team. Signals: "RFP written with competitor in mind" → ask directly then decide; "customer only talks price" → kill; "no decision-maker in room" → offer exec alignment, if refused → kill.

---

## MM-04: No Long Feedback Loops
**Source:** Annie Duke — `episodes/annie-duke/transcript.md`

Instead of waiting years for ultimate outcome, identify leading indicators that correlate with the desired outcome.

> "There is no such thing as a long feedback loop. And the way you choose to shorten the feedback loop is to say, what are the things that are correlated with the outcome that I eventually desire?" — Annie Duke

**Apply:** For multi-year bets, define 30/60/90-day proxy metrics.

## MM-05: Most Two-Way Doors Are One-Way (for PMs)
**Source:** Shreyas Doshi — `episodes/shreyas-doshi-live/transcript.md`

The "reversible decision" metaphor is dangerous because PMs apply it too liberally. Once a feature ships, gets presented at QBR, accumulates organizational commitment — it's irreversible.

> "Frankly, most doors that look like two-way doors are actually one-way doors. They are two-way doors at Bezos' level, but as a PM leader, for you, they are a one-way door, and that's making you busy." — Shreyas Doshi

> "Thinking is cheap, so you should do more thinking, not less." — Shreyas Doshi

---

## MM-06: Strategy Eliminates Planning Overhead
**Source:** Shreyas Doshi — `episodes/shreyas-doshi-live/transcript.md`

With real product strategy alignment, annual planning takes days not weeks. Most planning overhead exists because there's no real strategy.

> "I realized that actually, if you have a real product strategy, a real one that everybody is aligned with, that you have got pre-alignment on, then a lot of this nonsense we tend to do with annual planning actually goes away." — Shreyas Doshi

## MM-07: Taste in Beliefs
**Source:** Shreyas Doshi — `episodes/shreyas-doshi-live/transcript.md`

We obsess over product taste (pixels, UX) but ignore "taste in beliefs" — the quality of ideas we adopt. We're seduced by catchy metaphors, alliterations, authority bias.

> "We get overly excited about cool metaphors... What if that thing were called 'fail quickly'? Do you think you would be as attracted to that idea if it were called 'fail quickly'? Probably not, so what changed? The only thing that changed is one is an alliteration." — Shreyas Doshi

## MM-08: Test Everything + Portfolio of Bets
**Source:** Ronny Kohavi — `episodes/ronny-kohavi/transcript.md`

Any code change should be an experiment. 80-92% of ideas fail. Win through volume: many incremental experiments + some high-risk bets. Document learnings as institutional memory.

> "Of these experiments, 92% failed to improve the metric that we were trying to move. So only 8% of our ideas actually were successful at moving the key metrics." — Ronny Kohavi

**Case Study — Bing $100M:** A trivial UI change (moving ad title line) worth $100M/year sat in backlog for months. An engineer built it in 2 days. "We are often humbled by how bad we are at predicting the outcome of experiments."

**Case Study — Bing Social (100 person-years):** Hundreds of experiments showed flat/negative results. Eventually killed, but took too long.

## MM-09: Bias for Action (70% Rule)
**Source:** Ethan Evans — `episodes/ethan-evans/transcript.md`

Speed matters. Decide with 70% of the info you wish you had. In competitive markets, being right is good but being quick is necessary.

> "Bias for action says speed matters in business and many decisions are reversible. And so it's important to go faster." — Ethan Evans

> "In a competitive environment, being right is good, but being quick is necessary. Because if there are 10 startups working on an idea, some of them will gamble and be right." — Ethan Evans

---

## MM-10: Disagree and Commit (Done Right)
**Source:** Bill Carr — `episodes/bill-carr/transcript.md`

NOT "I disagree but I'll do it anyway." The disagree phase is an OBLIGATION to bring new information. Commit means genuinely understanding the reasoning and running with it.

> "Good decisions are made by first understanding all the different points of view and pros and cons... great leaders, what they do is they solicit these different points of views." — Bill Carr

---

## MM-11: Brain Departments (History vs Science vs Art)
**Source:** Evan LaPointe — `episodes/evan-lapointe/transcript.md`

The brain defaults to the "history department" (pattern-matching from past experience). Better decisions come from deliberately routing to "science department" (experimentation) or "art department" (creative alternatives).

> "The brain is like a college campus that has different departments in it. Most people rely on their history department way too much." — Evan LaPointe

**Apply for meetings:** Prime before deciding. Establish principles before tactics. If one person optimizes for speed and another for accuracy, conflict is inevitable.

---

## MM-12: The Unquantifiable Advantage
**Source:** Tobi Lutke — `episodes/tobi-lutke/transcript.md`

~80% of value space is unquantifiable (taste, quality, fun, delight). Companies that are comfortable with unquantifiable things access this value. Goodhart's Law makes pure metrics self-defeating.

> "I just think the overlap of most valuable things you can do with a product, and for things that happen to be fully quantifiable, it's like maybe 20% which leaves 80% of a value space unaddressable by the people who will only look at quantifiable things." — Tobi Lutke

> "Optimism always sounds dumb, or at least naive. Pessimism sounds extremely sophisticated. Metrics driven sounds extremely sophisticated. Talking about fun sounds like naive." — Tobi Lutke

---

## MM-13: Opportunity Solution Tree
**Source:** Teresa Torres — `episodes/teresa-torres/transcript.md`

Start from outcome → branch into opportunity space (needs/pains) → branch into solutions → branch into assumption tests. Prevents jumping from outcome to solution.

> "Most product teams don't really know how to manage this really complex problem of how do I start from an outcome and figure out what to build?" — Teresa Torres
